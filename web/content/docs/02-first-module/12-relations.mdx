relation types, and enforce who may touch which order. · **Time:** ~3 h ·
**Checkpoint:** `code/checkpoints/ch12`

## Why this matters

A fleet of unconnected vehicles is a spreadsheet. The moment a vehicle knows its
owner, an order knows its vehicle, and a technician knows their orders, you have a
business application. Relations are where the ORM earns its keep, and they're also
where new Odoo developers make their most expensive mistakes (wrong `ondelete`,
inverted one2many, record rules that lock out the boss). This chapter builds
LibreFleet's centerpiece, the **service order**, wires all three relation types
around it, and pays the debt from chapter 10: the record rule that limits
technicians to their own work.

## Concepts

### Three relation types, one foreign key story

- **`Many2one`** is *the* relation; the other two are conveniences built around it.
  `vehicle_id = fields.Many2one("librefleet.vehicle")` becomes a plain integer
  column holding the other record's id, with a real foreign key constraint in
  Postgres. Naming convention: singular, `_id` suffix.
- **`One2many`** is a Many2one read from the other end. `service_order_ids` on the
  vehicle is *not* a column anywhere: it's a live query ("all orders whose
  `vehicle_id` is me"), which is why declaring one always names the inverse field:
  `fields.One2many("librefleet.service.order", "vehicle_id")`. No Many2one, no
  One2many. Naming: plural, `_ids` suffix.
- **`Many2many`** links both ways without a "belongs to" side. Technicians work on
  many orders, orders can need two mechanics. In Postgres this becomes a separate
  *relation table* of id pairs that Odoo names and manages for you.

### `ondelete`: decide now what deletion means

Every Many2one carries an `ondelete` policy, and the default (`set null`) is a
decision too. LibreFleet uses all three settings deliberately:

| Field | Policy | Because |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `line.part_id` | `set null` (default) | a deleted catalog part shouldn't destroy history |
| `order.vehicle_id` | `restrict` | an order without its vehicle is meaningless; block the delete |
| `line.order_id` | `cascade` | lines are part of their order; delete the order, the lines go too |

These become real `ON DELETE` clauses in Postgres, so they hold even against raw
SQL. You'll read them straight out of `\d` in the hands-on.

### Record rules: the per-record layer, at last

Chapter 10 promised it: ACLs answer "may technicians write orders at all" (yes),
record rules answer "*which* orders". An `ir.rule` attaches a domain to a model for
some groups; the ORM silently appends it to every read, write, create or unlink the
rule is flagged for (`perm_read`, `perm_write`...).

One behavior surprises everyone once, so learn it on purpose: **rules from all your
groups combine with OR, and having *any* rule on a model means users without a
matching rule get nothing.** Workshop managers are also Workshop users (chapter
10's `implied_ids`), so a technicians-only rule catches managers too, unless the
manager group gets its own rule saying "everything" (`[(1, '=', 1)]`). The
hands-on walks you into that trap with your own admin account, then out of it.

### x2many values: the command list

You'll meet writes like `technician_ids: [(4, tina.id)]` in this chapter's shell
work (you already saw one in chapter 10's group XML). Relational fields are written
with a small command language: `(4, id)` links an existing record, `(0, 0, {vals})`
creates one attached to the parent, `(6, 0, [ids])` replaces the whole set,
`(3, id)`/`(2, id)` unlink or delete one. Since Odoo 15 you can also write them
readably as `Command.link(id)`, `Command.create(vals)` and so on; the tuple form is
what you'll encounter in twenty years of existing code, so learn to read both.

## Hands-on

### 1. The service order and its lines

`code/addons/librefleet/models/service_order.py`, two models in one file because
lines never live apart from their order:

```python
from odoo import fields, models


class ServiceOrder(models.Model):
    _name = "librefleet.service.order"
    _description = "Service Order"
    _rec_name = "vehicle_id"
    _order = "scheduled_start desc, id desc"

    vehicle_id = fields.Many2one(
        "librefleet.vehicle", required=True, ondelete="restrict", index=True)
    service_type_id = fields.Many2one("librefleet.service.type")
    technician_ids = fields.Many2many("res.users", string="Technicians")
    line_ids = fields.One2many("librefleet.service.order.line", "order_id")
    stage = fields.Selection(
        [
            ("draft", "Draft"),
            ("confirmed", "Confirmed"),
            ("in_progress", "In Progress"),
            ("done", "Done"),
            ("cancelled", "Cancelled"),
        ],
        default="draft", required=True)
    scheduled_start = fields.Datetime()
    scheduled_end = fields.Datetime()
    notes = fields.Text()


class ServiceOrderLine(models.Model):
    _name = "librefleet.service.order.line"
    _description = "Service Order Line"

    order_id = fields.Many2one(
        "librefleet.service.order", required=True, ondelete="cascade", index=True)
    part_id = fields.Many2one("librefleet.part")
    qty = fields.Float(string="Quantity", default=1.0)
    price_unit = fields.Float(string="Unit Price")
```

New pieces beyond the relations themselves: **`Selection`** stores one key from a
closed list (the string next to each key is the label users see); the order's
lifecycle lives here until chapter 17 turns it into a statusbar. `index=True` on
the heavily-searched Many2ones adds a real database index. `_rec_name =
"vehicle_id"` borrows the vehicle's display name until chapter 14 gives orders a
proper reference. And until chapter 13 computes them, prices sit as plain floats.

### 3. Close the loop on the vehicle

Two lines in `vehicle.py`, right after `license_plate`:

```python
    owner_id = fields.Many2one("res.partner", string="Owner")
    service_order_ids = fields.One2many("librefleet.service.order", "vehicle_id")
```

`res.partner` is core Odoo's universal person/company model; linking to it instead

composes with yours for free.

### 4. ACLs for three new models

Six new lines in `security/ir.model.access.csv`, same pattern as always:
technicians read the parts catalog but don't edit it, and they work with orders and
lines but never delete an order (see the checkpoint for the exact lines). Note
what's *deliberately* granted: user create on orders and full line access; the real
restriction comes next.

### 5. The record rule, and the trap

Append the technician rule to `security/librefleet_security.xml`, inside a
`<data noupdate="1">` block (rules are policy, not code; `noupdate` keeps a later
upgrade from overwriting what a client tuned in production; chapter 24 returns to
this):

```xml
    <data noupdate="1">

        <record id="rule_service_order_technician" model="ir.rule">
            <field name="name">Service orders: technicians handle their own</field>
            <field name="model_id" ref="model_librefleet_service_order"/>
            <field name="groups" eval="[(4, ref('group_librefleet_user'))]"/>
            <field name="domain_force">[('technician_ids', 'in', user.ids)]</field>
            <field name="perm_read" eval="False"/>
            <field name="perm_write" eval="True"/>
            <field name="perm_create" eval="True"/>
            <field name="perm_unlink" eval="True"/>
        </record>

    </data>
```

`domain_force` is evaluated with `user` in scope; `perm_read=False` means reading
stays unrestricted (technicians see the whole planning, they just can't touch
foreign orders). Upgrade, then seed some real data from the shell: two partners as
owners, three parts, and two orders, one with tina assigned, one without. The full
paste-ready snippet is `code/checkpoints/ch12/seed_ch12.py`; the line worth
studying is the one2many write:

```python
o1 = env["librefleet.service.order"].create({
    "vehicle_id": zh.id,
    "service_type_id": oil_change.id,
    "technician_ids": [(4, tina.id)],
    "scheduled_start": "2026-07-16 08:00:00",
    "scheduled_end": "2026-07-16 09:00:00",
    "line_ids": [
        (0, 0, {"part_id": oil_filter.id, "qty": 1, "price_unit": 24.5}),
        (0, 0, {"part_id": oil.id, "qty": 4, "price_unit": 18.9}),
    ],
})
```

Commit, then walk into the trap. As **admin**, try to confirm the order tina is
*not* on:

```console
>>> o2.with_user(env.ref("base.user_admin")).write({"stage": "confirmed"})
AccessError: Uh-oh! Looks like you have stumbled upon some top-secret records.

Sorry, Administrator (id=2) doesn't have 'write' access to:
- Service Order (librefleet.service.order)
```

The boss is locked out of the workshop. Why: admin is a Workshop Manager, Manager
implies User, so the technician rule applies to admin, and admin is in
`technician_ids` of nothing. This is the most common record-rule bug in the wild,
and now you've caused it yourself. The fix is the standard pattern, a second rule
that gives managers everything:

```xml
        <record id="rule_service_order_manager" model="ir.rule">
            <field name="name">Service orders: managers handle all</field>
            <field name="model_id" ref="model_librefleet_service_order"/>
            <field name="groups" eval="[(4, ref('group_librefleet_manager'))]"/>
            <field name="domain_force">[(1, '=', 1)]</field>
        </record>
```

Upgrade, retest: admin writes any order (rules OR together, and `[(1, '=', 1)]`
matches everything), while tina still bounces off foreign orders. The server log
narrates every denial:

```text
INFO tutorial odoo.addons.base.models.ir_rule: Access Denied by record rules for operation: write on record ids: [2], uid: 2, model: librefleet.service.order
```

Nothing conceptually new, so skim the checkpoint for the full XML:

```xml
    <field name="technician_ids" widget="many2many_tags"/>
    ...
    <field name="line_ids" nolabel="1">
        <list editable="bottom">
            <field name="part_id"/>
            <field name="qty"/>
            <field name="price_unit"/>
```

`widget=` picks a different renderer for the same data (tags beat a full table for

### 7. Read the relations back from Postgres

```console
$ docker compose exec db psql -U odoo -d tutorial -c '\d librefleet_service_order_line'
...
Foreign-key constraints:
    "librefleet_service_order_line_order_id_fkey" FOREIGN KEY (order_id) REFERENCES librefleet_service_order(id) ON DELETE CASCADE
    "librefleet_service_order_line_part_id_fkey" FOREIGN KEY (part_id) REFERENCES librefleet_part(id) ON DELETE SET NULL
```

Your `ondelete=` choices, verbatim, as database constraints. The many2many is here
too, a table you never declared:

```console
$ docker compose exec db psql -U odoo -d tutorial -c "\dt *service_order*rel*"
 public | librefleet_service_order_res_users_rel | table | odoo
```

And `restrict` does its job even from the shell as superuser, because it lives in
the database, not in Python:

```console
>>> zh.unlink()
ForeignKeyViolation: update or delete on table "librefleet_vehicle" violates foreign key constraint "librefleet_service_order_vehicle_id_fkey" on table "librefleet_service_order"
```

(That's the raw violation; remember to `env.cr.rollback()` after it.)

### 8. Break-it lab: the inverse that isn't

One2many's inverse name is a string, and strings can lie. In `line_ids`, change
`"order_id"` to `"service_order_id"` and upgrade:

```text
ValueError: 'service_order_id' declared in 'librefleet.service.order.line_ids' does not exist on 'librefleet.service.order.line'.
```

This one fails at registry setup, before any XML loads, so the message is short and
surgical: the One2many names its comodel and the exact field it expected to find
there. When you meet it in real life it's usually a rename that only got done on
one side. Restore `"order_id"`, upgrade, breathe.

## Gotchas

- **A one2many is a view, not storage.** No column exists for
  `service_order_ids`; assigning to the Many2one is what creates the link. If a
  one2many "loses" records, look at the inverse field's values, not the one2many.
- **Rules on a model apply to everyone in *any* matching group, and silence means
  denial.** The manager lockout you built is the canonical case: adding a
  restrictive rule for a base group restricts every group that implies it. Pair
  restrictive rules with an explicit allow-all for the privileged group.
- **`ondelete` defaults to `set null`, which is sometimes data loss with extra
  steps.** An order line whose order vanished is garbage; a vehicle losing its
  owner reference is fine. Decide per field, and remember `restrict` is enforced
  by Postgres itself.
- **`noupdate="1"` cuts both ways.** Your record rules won't be clobbered by
  upgrades, but your *own* domain fixes won't apply either; during development,
  uninstall/reinstall or edit the rule in the UI, and revisit this in chapter 24.
- **Commit before switching hats.** Shell experiments with `with_user` on
  uncommitted records can confuse you: the rule check happens against what the
  cursor sees, but a rollback after an AccessError also throws away your seed
  data. Seed, commit, then test permissions.
